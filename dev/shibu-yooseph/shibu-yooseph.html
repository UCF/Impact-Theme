“The chance to address big and important scientific questions and to train the next generation of scientists is a fantastic one.”

- Shibu Yooseph

Shibu Yooseph came to UCF for the opportunity to build a strong multidisciplinary academic and research program in the burgeoning field of genomics and bioinformatics. As a leader in the university’s Faculty Research Cluster initiative, the computer scientist who contributed to the first sequencing of the human genome is helping UCF pioneer new solutions to the world’s greatest challenges.

“Genomics and Bioinformatics, which is at the interface of the traditional biological sciences and computational sciences, is well on its way to becoming its own discipline and has been proving to be fundamental in teasing out the complexities of life and offering solutions to vast and diverse challenges like human health and sustainable energy.”

“My research has been very cross-disciplinary to-date, involving collaborations and applications in the fields of computing, genomics, microbiology, environmental research and biomedical sciences. UCF has strong programs in these areas, and the opportunity to connect these disciplines to address big and important scientific questions and to train the next generation of scientists is a fantastic one.”

“By their very nature, faculty research clusters span different disciplines. Done right, they have the potential to be greater than the sum of their parts. Faculty research clusters allow development of new fields and disciplines that are currently at the interface of traditional disciplines, and to tackle problems that may not be fully within the reach of a single discipline by itself.”

About Shibu

Education
1992 Bachelor of Technology in Computer Science and Engineering, Institute of Technology, Banaras Hindu University, India
1997 Ph.D. in Computer and Information Science, University of Pennsylvania.
Resume
1997-98 Postdoctoral fellow, Center for Discrete Mathematics and Theoretical Computer Science (DIMACS), Rutgers University
1998-00 Consultant, Lucent Technologies, Nokia Bell Labs), Murray Hill during this period.
1998-00 Research associate, Department of Mathematics, University of Southern California
2000-03 Computer scientist, Informatics Research Group, Celera Genomics and Applied Biosystems
2003-16 Faculty, J. Craig Venter Institute
2014-16 Senior Director, Human Longevity, Inc.
2016 Professor, Computer Science, College of Engineering and Computer Science, UCF — Lead, UCF Genomics and Bioinformatics Research Cluster
Specialties
Yooseph’s current focus is in the human microbiome area, with a goal of understanding host-microbiome associations/interactions in context of human diseases. In addition, he designs algorithms for genome and metagenome data analysis, biomarker identification, sequence assembly, phylogenetics, microbial population modeling, pathway identification, and ‘-omics’ data integration.

Genomics and Bioinformatics Faculty Cluster

The goal in creating the genomics and bioinformatics faculty cluster is to inspire research that leverages UCF’s strengths in medicine, evolution and ecology, and computer sciences by providing the technical expertise and collaborative opportunities necessary to ask and answer the most important questions of today’s life science. Examples include understanding the genetic basis and evolution of diseases, identifying emerging pathogens, determining genomic hallmarks of species extinctions, and developing algorithms and statistical models to improve the analysis of these fantastical amounts of data.
